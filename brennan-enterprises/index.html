When leads cross two metros and several product lines, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-showroom shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the windows going in. At Brennan's reach, an inbound lead can come from either metro and any of several product and channel lines (replacement, design-build, new construction, supply-only), and a window or door job then runs through a long cycle: an in-home measure, a quote, a sale, an order, a manufacturing lead time, an install schedule, and a final walkthrough. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the market the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the product line it belongs to, the jump from sales to the install calendar. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job and booking the next install. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-market, multi-product operation creates.

The seam: leads and jobs that stall between markets, product lines, and the install handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across two metros and several product lines and runs them through a multi-step measure-to-install cycle (measure, quote, sale, order, manufacturing lead time, install schedule, final walkthrough), a fraction of leads and sold jobs stall in the gaps: a quote that never gets a second touch and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong metro or product line and never picked back up, or a sold order that never cleanly reaches the install calendar. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across markets, brands, and channels, you cannot see which channel, market, or product line the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. When demand spikes seasonally, volume climbs, which is exactly when handoffs slip the most. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy two-market operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, two markets, and several product lines at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across two metros the years add up.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market (showroom region). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market or product-line tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel, metro, or product line is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for quotes that went quiet without a second touch, and for sold orders that never moved into a scheduled install. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-job by lead source, split by market and product line. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, product line, and the install stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
