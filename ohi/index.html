Two front doors into one production line is where leads quietly stall.

At a one-person shop, the same person sells the job and builds it. At OHi's size, a lead comes in through one of two front-end teams: residential design and sales for kitchens, baths, and additions, or commercial sales and design for healthcare, student housing, and multi-family work. Both close their leads, and both then hand the job into the same shared production and project-management team. That shared handoff is a structural seam: a residential job can sit waiting behind commercial production capacity, or a commercial job can queue behind residential work, and neither team on its own can see the other side of the shared queue. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching both intake lines and the one shared production queue on one screen while everyone is busy with the next client. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that running two lines of business through one production team creates.

The seam: leads that stall behind the other line's work, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads from two different intake lines (residential design/sales and commercial sales/design) and both hand off into one shared production and project-management team, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a closed residential job that sits queued behind commercial production, a commercial lead that goes cold waiting for the next production slot, or a referral that is never tagged to the channel or line of business it actually came from. They die quietly. And because lead source and business line are not tagged cleanly end to end, you cannot see which channel, or which line of business, is actually paying for itself, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted marketing spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a two-line shop sharing one production team does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry leads cleanly across two lines of business and one shared production queue at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on jobs this size the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by business line (residential vs. commercial). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel, or which line, is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for leads that closed in sales but never cleanly entered the shared production queue, and for jobs from either line sitting idle waiting on a production slot. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-completed-job by lead source and by business line. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source and production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
