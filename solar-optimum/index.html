When leads cross four states and a long install cycle, the handoffs are where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop with one job type, one person can watch a lead from the first click to the system switching on. At Solar Optimum's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of four states and split into two very different paths: a residential solar and Powerwall job runs one way, a commercial or community solar project runs a completely different way. And both move through a long solar cycle, from first click to site survey, design, contract, permitting, install, inspection, and permission to operate. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the state the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from sales to design, the jump from a sold deal to install and PTO. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next deal. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-market, residential-and-commercial operation with a long install cycle creates.

The seam: leads that stall between states, channels, and the long install handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across four states (California, Nevada, Arizona, Florida) and two very different job types (a residential solar and battery install and a commercial or community solar project), through a long sell-to-PTO-to-install cycle, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a residential deal that goes quiet between design and permitting, a commercial bid that waits on a property owner and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong state or rep and never picked back up, or a sold job that never cleanly hands to install and PTO. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and states, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted acquisition spend never gets traced back. Solar customer-acquisition cost is high, so the stakes per lost lead are large, and a single stalled commercial or community-solar deal is worth many residential jobs. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and systems actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-market operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, four states, and a long install cycle all at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on solar-sized deals with high acquisition cost, the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by state (CA / NV / AZ / FL). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean state tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or market is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for deals that went quiet between design and permitting, commercial bids waiting on a property owner, and sold jobs that never moved into a scheduled install or PTO. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the commercial side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-system by lead source, split residential vs commercial. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, job type, and the install and PTO stages are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
