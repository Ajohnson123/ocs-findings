When leads cross five offices and a multi-week install cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-office shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the panels going on the roof. At Solar Energy Partners' reach, an inbound lead can land in any of five metros across two states, then run a long, staged path to become a turned-on system: lead, site survey, design, contract, permit, install, inspection, and finally utility permission to operate. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the office the lead routed to, the channel that brought it in, the jump from a signed contract to a scheduled install to PTO. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next deal. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-office, long-cycle solar operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between offices, channels, and the install pipeline, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across five field offices in two states (Georgia and South Carolina) and through a long, staged solar pipeline (site survey, design, contract, permit, install, inspection, utility PTO), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a lead routed to the wrong metro or consultant and never picked back up, a signed contract that sits for weeks waiting on permit or design and goes cold, or a project stuck between install and utility PTO that nobody circles back on. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and offices, you cannot see which channel or which office the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. On solar work the stakes per stalled deal are large, because each job is high-ticket and the cycle is long enough for a deal to go cold before anyone notices. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and systems actually turned on, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-office operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and several offices at once, through a pipeline that runs for weeks, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on high-ticket solar jobs the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by office or metro (Atlanta, Savannah, Charleston, Columbia, Greenville). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean office tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or office is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for signed contracts that went quiet waiting on permit or design, and for projects that stalled between install and utility PTO. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on solar each one is high-ticket.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-system by lead source, split by office. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, and the pipeline stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
