In a storm and insurance restoration model, the long claim cycle is where leads quietly stall.

At a one-truck shop, the same person knocks the door, runs the inspection, and books the job. At Elevated's size, a storm lead crosses several hands and a long cycle: it comes in from canvassing after a hail event, a referral, paid search, or a web request, then it has to be inspected, turned into an insurance claim, approved by the carrier, and only then scheduled into production, and it can split three ways because a residential roof, a commercial reroof, and an electrical job each run a different path. Every one of those steps is a seam, and the structure makes them sharper here than usual: the lead lives in your marketing-and-sales system, while the built job lives in your production-and-scheduling system, so a lead has to cross a system boundary and a division boundary on its way to becoming a finished roof. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy with the next storm. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the storm-restoration model creates.

The seam: leads that stall in the claim cycle and the production handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one lead-and-sales system carries every storm channel (post-hail canvassing, referrals, paid search, web forms, inspection bookings, adjuster referrals) and a lead has to survive a long claim cycle and cross into a separate production-and-scheduling system, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a claim that sits pending with the insurer and goes cold, an approved job that never gets cleanly scheduled into production, an inspection booked but never run, or a residential lead that is really a commercial or electrical job and never gets rerouted. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across all those channels, you cannot see which channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted canvassing and marketing spend never gets traced back. On the insurance and commercial side the stakes per lost lead are large, because a single stalled reroof or approved claim is worth many small repairs. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and roofs actually built, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-division storm operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across a long claim cycle, several channels, and a handoff into a second system, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on insurance-restoration jobs the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel (canvassing, referral, paid search, web) is actually paying for itself during a storm surge.
  2. Filter for claims that went pending with the insurer and stalled, for approved jobs that never moved into a scheduled production job, and for inspections booked but never run. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the claim cycle and the handoff between your sales side and your production side, and on the insurance and commercial side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-built-roof by lead source, split residential vs commercial. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, division, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
