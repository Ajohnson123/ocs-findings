When leads cross seven markets and run through a long install cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the panels going on. At Solar Energy World's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of seven markets, and a signed deal then runs through a long multi-party cycle: a site survey, a system design, a proposal, a financing approval, a permit, a utility interconnection, an install, an inspection, and final permission to operate. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the market the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, each step of the project, the jump from sales to install. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next deal and clearing the next backlog. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-market, long-cycle install operation creates.

The seam: leads and deals that stall between markets, channels, and the install handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across seven markets and runs them through a long sales-to-install cycle (design, financing, permit, utility interconnection, install, inspection, permission to operate), a fraction of leads and signed deals stall in the gaps: a deal that waits on a permit or an interconnection approval and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong market or rep and never picked back up, or a signed contract that never cleanly hands to install. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and markets, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. When an incentive deadline or a busy season hits, volume spikes, which is exactly when handoffs slip the most. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and systems actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy seven-market operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several markets, and several project steps at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across seven markets the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or location is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for signed deals that went quiet waiting on a permit or an interconnection approval, and for leads that never moved from sales into a scheduled install. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-job by lead source, split by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, project stage, and the install stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
