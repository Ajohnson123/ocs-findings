When storm leads cross several markets and split into a claim path and a retail path, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop with one job type, one person can watch a lead from the first knock to the crew rolling off the finished roof. At Roofsmith's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of several markets and from any of several storm channels, then split into two very different paths: a retail re-roof runs one way, and an insurance-restoration job runs a completely different way through the inspection, the adjuster, the scope, the supplement, the approval, and only then production. On top of that, residential and commercial each run their own way. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the market the lead came from, the storm event and channel that produced it, the jump from a claim into a scheduled job. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy chasing the next storm. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a several-market, storm-driven, insurance-restoration operation creates.

The seam: storm leads that stall between markets, the claim, and the production handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries storm and retail leads across several metro markets and two very different paths (a retail re-roof and an insurance-restoration claim), for both residential and commercial, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a claim that goes quiet waiting on an adjuster or a supplement, a storm-canvass lead routed to the wrong market or never worked, a commercial bid that goes cold, or a signed job that never cleanly hands to production. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across storm channels (canvassing after a hail or wind event, referrals, web, insurance referrals), markets, and the two tracks, you cannot see which storm, channel, or market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the money spent chasing that storm never gets traced back. Storm volume spikes hard right after each event, which is exactly when a lead is most likely to slip. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and roofs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy several-market storm operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several markets, and two tracks at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs storm after storm, and the lost roofs add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market and by track (retail vs insurance restoration). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market or track tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which storm, channel, or market is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for insurance-restoration claims that went quiet waiting on an adjuster or a supplement, and for leads that never moved from a signed job into a scheduled production job. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and after a storm each one is a full roof.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split by market and track. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, track, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
