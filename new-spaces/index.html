A likely silent seam in New Spaces' lead flow

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location field-services shape you run, just a messier version of it. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools a remodeling company runs on. You clearly run a real operation: a second-generation design-build remodeler that has served the Twin Cities for four decades, with an Edina design center, a Burnsville production side, and an award record to match, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at New Spaces' public footprint (that long history, the range of lead channels from home shows to Houzz to NARI referrals to repeat clients, a separate design center and production team, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside while a company is growing and winning awards, even for a sharp team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

In a design-build model, the handoff between steps is where leads quietly stall.

At a one-person shop, the same person sells the job and builds it. At New Spaces' size, a lead crosses several steps: marketing brings it in from a home show, Houzz, a NARI referral, the web, or a past client, a consultation qualifies it, the design center develops it into a signed design agreement and project, and only then does it hand to production. Every one of those handoffs is a seam, and the structure makes them sharper here than usual: the design center and the production side sit in different places and different hands, so a lead has to travel from inquiry, through the Edina design center, into a signed project, and over to the Burnsville production side to become a built remodel. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy with the next client. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the design-build model creates.

The seam: leads that stall between the consultation and the signed project, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries every lead channel (home shows, Houzz, NARI referral, web, repeat client) and a lead has to travel from a first inquiry, through the design center, into a signed project, and over to production, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a consultation that never books a design agreement, a design that stalls before the project is signed, or a warm referral that goes cold waiting on the next step. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, you cannot see which channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted marketing spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and projects actually signed and built, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a multi-step design-build shop does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs and several channels at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on jobs this size the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel (home shows, Houzz, NARI referral, web, repeat client) is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for leads that had a consultation but never booked a design agreement, and for warm referrals with no recent activity. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between the consultation, the design center, and the signed project.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-signed-project by lead source. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source and the design or production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
