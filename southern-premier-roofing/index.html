A likely silent seam in Southern Premier Roofing's lead flow

When leads cross many offices and four kinds of work, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-office, single-job-type shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the crew rolling off the finished job. At Southern Premier's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of about ten offices across several states, and split into four very different paths: a residential re-roof runs one way, a commercial roof on an office or retail building runs a completely different way through a longer cycle with a property manager or a general contractor, a storm or insurance restoration job runs a third way through an adjuster and a claim, and a gutter or exterior job runs a fourth. Then every one of those has to hand cleanly from where the lead is captured to where the job is actually produced and scheduled. Every one of those points is a seam: the office the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the kind of work it is, and the jump from a captured lead into a scheduled production job. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-office, multi-line operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between offices, job types, and the production handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot captures leads across roughly ten offices and several states and four very different kinds of work (a residential re-roof, a commercial roof, a storm or insurance restoration job, and a gutter or exterior job), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a commercial bid waiting on a property manager or a GC that goes cold, a storm or insurance job stuck waiting on an adjuster that nobody circles back on, a lead captured in one office that never routes cleanly to the right crew, or a sold job that never cleanly hands from the lead system into production and scheduling. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, offices, and job types, you cannot see which channel, office, or line of work the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted ad spend never gets traced back. On commercial and storm-restoration work the stakes per lost lead are large, because a single stalled commercial roof or insurance claim is worth many small repairs. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-office operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several offices, and several kinds of work at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on commercial and storm-restoration jobs the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by office and job type (residential / commercial / storm-insurance / gutters). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean office or job-type tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel, office, or line of work is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for commercial and storm-restoration leads that went quiet waiting on a property manager, GC, or adjuster, and for leads that never moved from the lead system into a scheduled production job. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the commercial and insurance side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split by office and job type. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, job type, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
