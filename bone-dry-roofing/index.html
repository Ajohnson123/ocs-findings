When leads cross fifteen markets and run through an insurance-claims cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the roof going on. At Bone Dry's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of fifteen markets, and a storm job then runs through a long multi-party cycle: an adjuster appointment, a claim approval, a supplement request, a material release, an install, and a final payment. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the market the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, each step of the claim, the jump from sales to production. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job and chasing the next storm. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-market, insurance-restoration operation creates.

The seam: leads and claims that stall between markets, channels, and the insurance handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across fifteen markets in seven-plus states and runs them through a multi-step insurance-claims cycle (adjuster, claim approval, supplement, material release, install, final payment), a fraction of leads and open claims stall in the gaps: a claim that waits on an adjuster or a supplement and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong market or branch and never picked back up, or a sold job that never cleanly hands to production. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and markets, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. After a storm, volume spikes, which is exactly when handoffs slip the most. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy fifteen-location operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several markets, and several claim steps at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across fifteen markets the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or location is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for open insurance claims that went quiet waiting on an adjuster or a supplement, and for leads that never moved from sales into a scheduled production job. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, claim stage, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
