In a multi-branch, recurring-contract model, the handoffs between branches are where revenue quietly leaks.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call through the bid, the signed contract, and every renewal after that. At Michael Hatcher's reach, a lead comes in across many channels and many client types, then has to be routed to the right branch and the right brand by service area, turned into a bid, won as a contract, and then renewed every year after that. Every one of those steps is a handoff, and the multi-branch, multi-brand structure makes them sharper here than usual: a lead has to land at the right branch, a bid has to be owned by someone, a one-time install or enhancement has to be converted into a recurring contract, and a recurring contract has to be watched as its renewal date comes up, branch by branch. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path across every branch on one screen while everyone is busy with the next account. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the multi-branch, recurring-contract model creates.

The seam: leads and contracts that stall between branches and at renewal, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries every lead across three metros, multiple divisions, and two customer-facing brands (commercial maintenance, design-build, irrigation, and the lawn-care brand) into a recurring-contract model, a fraction of leads and accounts stall in the gaps: a bid that goes out from one branch and is never followed up because it is unclear who owns it, a proposal sent to the wrong service area or wrong brand and never re-routed, a one-time install or enhancement that is never converted into a recurring maintenance contract, and the quiet one, an annual maintenance contract that silently lapses at renewal because no single screen is watching the renewal date across branches. They die quietly. And because lead source, branch, and brand ownership are not tagged cleanly, you cannot see which channel, branch, or brand the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted bid and marketing spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and contracts actually held and renewed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a multi-branch grounds-management company does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several branches, several brands, several handoffs, and a renewal cycle at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on a recurring-contract model the years compound, because a single lapsed contract costs every year it stays gone.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by branch and brand. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clear branch or brand owner, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel, branch, or brand is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for open bids and proposals with no activity in 30+ days, and for one-time install or enhancement jobs that never became a recurring maintenance contract. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the branch and bid handoffs.
  3. Ask HubSpot for contracts coming up for renewal in the next 90 days, by branch. If that report cannot be built cleanly because renewal dates and branch ownership are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the silent-lapse leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
