A likely silent seam in Supreme Roofing's bid flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location field-services shape you run, just a messier version of it. So I spend my time on the silent seams between the tools a roofing company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, 38 years and four offices out of Dallas, Oklahoma City, Denver, and New England, carrying commercial work nationwide, from six-figure new construction and reroofs to maintenance and service, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at Supreme's public footprint (those four offices, that commercial mix, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a busy shop, even for a seasoned team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When bids cross four offices and a long commercial cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the roof going on. At Supreme's reach, an inbound lead or bid can come from any of four metros and split into very different paths: a small service or repair call runs one way, a six-figure reroof or new-construction job runs a completely different way, through a long cycle with an estimate, an owner or GC award decision, and a production schedule, and a recurring maintenance contract runs on a slip-prone cadence of its own. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from estimating to award, the jump from sale to production. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a four-office, long-cycle commercial operation creates.

The seam: bids that stall between offices, channels, and the production handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads and bids across four offices (Dallas, Oklahoma City, Denver, New England) and several very different work types (a quick service call and a six-figure reroof or new build), a fraction of bids stall in the gaps: a commercial bid that waits on an owner or GC award and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong office or person and never picked back up, a sale that never cleanly hands to production, or a maintenance renewal that quietly lapses. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and offices, you cannot see which channel or which office the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. On commercial work the stakes per stalled bid are large, because a single lost reroof is worth many service calls, and the long cycle gives a bid plenty of time to go quiet. The loss only shows up in the gap between bids worked and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy four-office operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a bid cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and several offices at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on commercial-sized jobs the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead and bid from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by office (Dallas / OKC / Denver / New England). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean office tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or office is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for commercial bids that went quiet waiting on an owner or GC award, and for jobs that never moved from sold into a scheduled production stage. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the commercial side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split by office and by work type. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, work type, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
