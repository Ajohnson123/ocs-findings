A likely silent seam in MOSS's lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same field-services shape you run of one CRM, multiple offices, and many lead channels feeding one funnel, just a messier version of it. I spend my time on the seams between the tools a home-services company runs on. You clearly run a real operation: the largest full-service design-build firm in DC, Maryland, and Northern Virginia, three straight Chrysalis Awards for Remodeling Excellence, two offices covering three jurisdictions, roughly 100 high-end projects a year, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at MOSS's public footprint (that award record, two office addresses across a tri-state metro, a wide range of lead channels), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside while a company is growing and winning awards, even for a sharp team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

Two offices and three jurisdictions is exactly where a lead can land in the wrong place and sit.

At a one-office shop, every lead lands in one place and one team works it end to end. At MOSS's size, a lead can come from DC, Maryland, or Northern Virginia, through Houzz, Angi, a referral, or your own award coverage, and has to be routed to the right office and design team before marketing, sales, design, and production can even start their handoff chain. Every one of those routing decisions and every one of those team handoffs is a seam, and having two physical offices makes it sharper than a single-location firm: a lead can land with the wrong office, or land correctly but stall crossing from a marketing-and-sales motion into design and then into production. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while both offices are busy with the next client. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot the two-office, tri-jurisdiction model creates.

The seam: leads lost to routing and handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot has to carry every lead channel across two offices and three jurisdictions, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a lead that lands with the wrong office and sits before anyone claims it, a qualified lead that closes but never cleanly enters the design queue, or a design that stalls before it reaches production. They die quietly. And because lead source and originating office are not both tagged cleanly, you cannot see which channel or which office the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the marketing spend behind it never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and projects actually built, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a two-office, multi-team design-build shop does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across an office boundary, several team handoffs, and several channels at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on jobs this size the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by originating office. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or unassigned on either field, attribution and routing are both blurry.
  2. Filter for leads that closed in sales but never cleanly entered the design or production queue, and for leads that sat unclaimed for more than a day or two after intake. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in routing or in the handoff between teams.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-built-project by lead source and by office. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, and production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
