When leads and bids cross several offices and run through a long commercial cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-office shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the roof going on. At West Roofing Systems' reach, an inbound lead or bid can come from any of several offices, and a commercial job then runs through a long multi-step cycle: an inspection, an estimate, a proposal, an award, a schedule, an install, and a closeout. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the office the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, each step of the bid, the jump from sales to production scheduling. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job and chasing the next bid. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-office, commercial bid operation creates.

The seam: leads and bids that stall between offices, channels, and the production handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads and bids across several offices in several states and runs them through a long commercial bid cycle (inspection, estimate, proposal, award, schedule, install, closeout), a fraction of leads and open bids stall in the gaps: a proposal sent and never followed up that goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong office or rep and never picked back up, or an awarded job that never cleanly hands to production scheduling. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and offices, you cannot see which channel or which office the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. Commercial bids have long cycles, so a stalled proposal can sit for months before anyone notices it went nowhere. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads and bids worked and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-office operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several offices, and several bid steps at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and when each lost job is a commercial roof, the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead and bid from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by office (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean office tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or office is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for proposals that were sent and then went quiet with no follow-up, and for awarded jobs that never moved from sales into a scheduled production job. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split by office. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, bid stage, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
