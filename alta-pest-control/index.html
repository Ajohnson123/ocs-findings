A likely silent seam in Alta Pest Control's lead flow

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location, in-home, field-services shape Alta runs, just a messier version of it. Day to day I map the silent seams between the tools a field-services company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, built from a Seattle garage in 2013 into 17 metro markets across the country, residential pest, mosquito, and termite work, growing fast, with leads coming in through HubSpot and the field running through a separate operations platform. Looking only at Alta's public footprint (that branch count, that recurring-plan model, leads captured on HubSpot and dispatched through a different field-ops system), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a fast-growing shop, even for a sharp team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When leads cross many branches and then jump systems, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the first service on the calendar. At Alta's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of 17 metro markets, then it has to make two jumps: out of the lead system and into field operations as a scheduled first service, and then from that first service into a recurring plan that bills month after month. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from the lead system into field ops, and the conversion into a recurring subscription. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy booking the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-branch, recurring-revenue operation running two systems creates.

The seam: leads that stall between branches, between systems, and between the first service and the recurring plan

When one lead system carries high-volume inbound across 17 markets and then has to hand off into a separate field-operations system and finally into a recurring subscription plan, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a lead routed to the wrong branch or never picked back up, a quote that never becomes a scheduled first service, or a sold customer whose recurring plan quietly lapses after the first or second visit. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across branches and channels, and because the lead system and the field-ops system are two different worlds, you cannot see which branch or channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted acquisition spend never gets traced back. On a recurring-plan business the stakes are larger than they look, because a silently churned customer is not a one-time loss, it is a multi-year stream that simply stops. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and recurring plans actually running, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-branch operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where a lead has to cross several branches, two separate systems, and one subscription conversion cleanly, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on a recurring-plan business the years compound, because every lost plan is revenue you keep not collecting every month after.

Roughly what it costs (public-data estimate)

Built from public figures (a fast-growing residential pest, mosquito, and termite operator across 17 metro markets; revenue treated as a conservative floor around $38M, which is the most recent reported figure and likely understates a business that grew roughly 400% over three years; a blended first-year customer value around $750, blending a $500 to $700 annual recurring pest plan with higher one-time termite and bed-bug jobs). Basis: ~$38M floor / ~$750 blended first-year value = roughly 50,000 active customers, and to sustain that plus the growth and replace normal plan churn the operation works on the order of 63,000 acquisition leads a year at a 30% close. Directional and estimate-grade, a floor meant to be made exact in an audit, and it counts first-year value only, so the recurring annuity on each lost customer is upside on top.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by branch / market. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean branch tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which branch or channel is actually paying for itself.
  2. Match those leads against the field-ops system: how many leads never became a scheduled first service, and of the customers who got a first service, how many never converted to or kept a recurring plan. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between the lead system, the field, and the subscription.
  3. Ask for cost-per-running-recurring-plan by lead source and by branch. If that report cannot be built cleanly because lead source, branch, the first scheduled service, and the recurring-plan status are not connected end to end across the two systems, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
