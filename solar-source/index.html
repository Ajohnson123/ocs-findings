A likely silent seam in Solar Source's lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location, field-services shape you run, just a messier version of it. So I spend my time on the seams between the tools a home-energy company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, founded in 1984, 60,000-plus installs, the largest residential solar installer in Florida, with two metros (Orlando and Tampa Bay), both residential and commercial, and solar, battery, pool heating, hot water, and EV charging all running on one HubSpot. Looking only at Solar Source's public footprint (that two-metro reach, that mix of residential and commercial work across several product lines, the long install cycle, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a busy shop, even for a seasoned team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When leads cross two metros, two segments, and a long install cycle, the handoffs are where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop with one product line, one person can watch a lead from the first click to the system switching on. At Solar Source's reach, an inbound lead can come from either metro, route to either office, and split across residential or commercial, and across several product lines: a solar and battery job runs one way, a pool-heating job another, a commercial project another still. And the solar work moves through a long cycle, from first click to site survey, design, contract, permitting, install, inspection, and permission to operate. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the metro the lead came from, the office it routed to, the segment it belongs to, the product line it belongs to, the jump from sales to design, the jump from a sold deal to install and PTO. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next deal. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a two-metro, multi-product operation with a long install cycle creates.

The seam: leads that stall between metros, segments, products, and the long install handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across two metros (Orlando and Tampa Bay), two offices (Altamonte Springs and Largo), two segments (residential and commercial), and several product lines (solar, battery, pool heating, hot water, EV charging), through a long sell-to-PTO-to-install cycle, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a solar deal that goes quiet between design and permitting, a lead routed to the wrong metro or product and never picked back up, a battery or pool-heating cross-sell that never gets re-touched, or a sold job that never cleanly hands to install and PTO. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, metros, and segments, you cannot see which channel, metro, or product the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted acquisition spend never gets traced back. Solar customer-acquisition cost is high, so the stakes per lost lead are large, and a single stalled full-system or commercial-solar deal is worth several smaller jobs. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and systems actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy two-metro operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, two metros, two segments, and a long install cycle all at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on solar-sized deals with high acquisition cost, the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source, by metro (Orlando / Tampa Bay), and by segment (residential / commercial). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean metro or segment tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel, metro, or product is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for solar deals that went quiet between design and permitting, leads routed to the wrong metro or product, and sold jobs that never moved into a scheduled install or PTO. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the full-system side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-system by lead source, split by metro and by product line. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, metro, product, and the install and PTO stages are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
