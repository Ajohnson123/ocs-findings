In a design-build model, the handoff between stages is where leads quietly stall.

At a one-person shop, the same person sells the job and builds it. At Sardone | McLain's size, a lead crosses several stages: the sales and design side brings it in from Houzz, a referral, the website, or a past client, and qualifies it, the design and estimate get developed, and only then does it hand to production to become a built project. Every one of those handoffs is a seam, and the structure makes them sharper here than usual: one owner sits on the sales-and-design side, while the other owner and the project managers sit on the production side, so a lead has to cross an internal boundary on its way to becoming a built project. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy with the next client. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the design-build process creates.

The seam: leads that stall between the first design conversation and production, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries every lead channel (Houzz, referral, website, past-client repeat, trade partner) and a lead has to cross from sales and design through estimating and into production, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a qualified inquiry that goes quiet after the first design consultation, a design or estimate that stalls before it reaches production, or a warm referral that goes cold waiting on the next stage to pick it up. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, you cannot see which channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted marketing spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and projects actually built, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a multi-stage design-build shop does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs and several channels at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on high-ticket design-build projects the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel (Houzz, referral, website, past-client, trade partner) is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for leads that had a first design consultation but never cleanly entered the design or production queue, and for warm referrals with no recent activity. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between stages.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-built-project by lead source. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
