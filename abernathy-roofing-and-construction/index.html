When leads cross two states and two job types, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-job-type shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the crew showing up. At Abernathy's reach, an inbound lead can come from either Missouri or Kansas and split into two very different paths: a storm-damage roof lead runs on an adjuster's timeline and needs a fast first touch, while a kitchen or bathroom remodel lead runs a slower, discretionary sales cycle with its own follow-up rhythm. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the state the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from first call to a job actually on the schedule. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy running the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a two-state, two-job-type operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between states, channels, and job types, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across two states (Missouri and Kansas) and two very different job types (insurance-driven roof work on an adjuster's clock, and discretionary remodel work on a slower sales cycle), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a storm-damage lead that sits too long before the first call and goes cold, a remodel inquiry routed to the wrong follow-up cadence and never revisited, or a lead that never cleanly moves from first call to a scheduled job. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and states, you cannot see which channel or which state the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually produced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy two-state operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and two states at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by state (Missouri / Kansas). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean state tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or area is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for storm/insurance leads that sat more than a day before the first outbound call, and for remodel inquiries that never moved into a follow-up sequence. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-produced-job by lead source, split roofing vs. remodel. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, job type, and production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
