A likely silent seam in EPM's lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I run the same core stack you do: HubSpot, inside a home-services operation. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools a pest management company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, 22 years, family-built, out of Columbus, serving Ohio, West Virginia, Kentucky, Indiana, and Michigan, with most of the business built on property-management and multi-family accounts rather than one-off residential visits, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at EPM's public footprint (that five-state reach, that mix of property-management and residential work, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a busy shop, even for a seasoned team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When leads cross five states and two very different buyer types, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-state residential-only shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the first treatment. At EPM's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of five states and split into two very different paths: a residential inquiry runs one way, a property-management RFP for an apartment community runs a completely different way, through a longer cycle with a decision-maker, a site walk-through, and a portfolio-level contract. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the state the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, whether it gets routed to the residential team or the commercial team. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy servicing the next property. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a five-state, B2B-and-residential operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between states, channels, and buyer types, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across five states (Ohio, West Virginia, Kentucky, Indiana, Michigan) and two very different buyer types (a property-management company weighing a portfolio-level contract, and a homeowner booking one visit), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a property-management RFP that waits on a site walk-through and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong state or team and never picked back up, or a residential inquiry that gets lost in a funnel built around the larger commercial motion. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across states and buyer types, you cannot see which channel or which state the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. On the property-management side the stakes per lost lead are large, because a single stalled portfolio contract is worth many residential jobs. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and contracts actually signed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy five-state operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and several states at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on property-management-sized contracts the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by state (OH / WV / KY / IN / MI). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean state tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or state is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for property-management RFPs that went quiet waiting on a site walk-through or a decision-maker, and for residential leads that never moved from inquiry into a booked first visit. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the property-management side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-signed-contract by lead source, split property-management vs residential. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, buyer type, and contract stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
