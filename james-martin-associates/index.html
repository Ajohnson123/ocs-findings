When leads cross offices and split into three kinds of work, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-office, single-service shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the crew showing up. At your reach, an inbound lead can come from either office, and it then has to be sorted into one of three very different motions: a recurring maintenance contract, a one-time design and build project, or a seasonal snow and ice agreement. After it is sold, it has to hand again, from the salesperson to a crew schedule. And a maintenance or snow contract has to be watched for renewal every season. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the office the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the division it belongs to, the jump from sales to a scheduled crew, the renewal date. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next contract and getting crews out the door. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-office, three-motion operation creates.

The seam: leads and jobs that stall between offices, divisions, the sales-to-crew handoff, and the seasonal renewal, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across more than one office and splits them across three different work motions (recurring maintenance contracts, project-based design and build, and seasonal snow and ice), a fraction of leads and sold jobs stall in the gaps: a lead routed to the wrong office or the wrong division and never picked back up, a maintenance or snow contract that quietly lapses at renewal, or a sold design and build job that never cleanly hands to a scheduled crew. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, offices, and the three motions, you cannot see which channel or which office the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. Seasonal snow adds a fresh renewal surface every single year, which widens the gap. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually crewed and delivered, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-office operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, more than one office, and three divisions at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across three work motions the years add up.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by office (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean office or division tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or location is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for leads that never got routed to the right office or division, for sold design and build jobs that never moved into a scheduled crew, and for maintenance or snow contracts that quietly lapsed at renewal. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-won-job by lead source, split by office and by division. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, office, division, and the crew-scheduling stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
