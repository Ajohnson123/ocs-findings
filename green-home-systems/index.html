A likely silent seam in Green Home Systems' lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential home-services operation, the same field-services shape you run, just a messier version of it. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools an energy and home-services company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, a fastest-growing, top-ranked solar and battery installer working across 21 states, moving a homeowner from first inquiry through design, permitting, install, and utility interconnection, all on one HubSpot, in market after market. Looking only at Green Home Systems' public footprint (that growth record and ranking, that range of lead channels from the web to referrals to past customers, 21 states and many utility territories, a hybrid model that hands the install to a local licensed contractor in many markets, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside while a company is growing fast and adding markets, even for a sharp team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

In solar, the long journey from signed to switched-on is where deals quietly stall.

At a one-truck shop, the same person sells the job and sees it through. At Green Home Systems' size, a deal crosses several stages: marketing and sales bring it in from the web, a referral, a past customer, or an event and qualify it, then it has to move through design, permitting, install, inspection, and the utility-interconnection queue before it reaches Permission To Operate and becomes activated revenue. Every one of those handoffs is a seam, and the structure makes them sharper here than usual: the work spans 21 states, so each deal's path depends on which market it is in (a different utility, a different county permit office, and in many markets a different local licensed contractor who actually does the install). In a hybrid model, a deal handed from your sales team to a local contractor crosses a company boundary, not just a desk. Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen across 21 markets while everyone is busy with the next customer. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the solar process and the multi-market model create.

The seam: deals that stall between the signed contract and switched-on, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries every lead channel and 21 markets (web, Google, referral, past customer, events, financing partners, across 21 states of utilities and permit offices) and a deal has to cross from sales and marketing through permitting, a local install partner, and utility interconnection, a fraction of deals stall in the gaps: a qualified web lead that goes quiet after the first design conversation, a signed contract that stalls in a local jurisdiction's permitting or the interconnection queue, or a deal handed to a local contractor in one market that goes cold waiting on the next stage. They die quietly. And because lead source and market are not tagged cleanly across all those channels and states, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted marketing and sales spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between deals signed and systems actually switched on, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a 21-state, multi-stage, hybrid-install solar operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a deal cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, 21 states of outside jurisdictions, and a per-market change of hands to a local contractor all at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on high-ticket solar and battery jobs, multiplied across 21 markets, the years add up fast.

Roughly what it costs (public-data estimate)

Built from public figures (a fastest-growing, top-ranked 21-state solar and battery installer with 1,000-plus installers and 11,000-plus customers; revenue treated as a conservative floor around $50M, which for a national installer this size likely understates it; a $20K to $45K and up average installed job, with solar-only running lower and solar-plus-battery running high). Basis: ~$50M floor / ~$28K average job = roughly 1,800 built projects a year, at about a 15% close that is roughly 12,000 inbound leads a year. Solar deals stall most at permitting and utility interconnection, which is the silent part of the journey, and the per-market contractor handoff adds a second stall point, so the leak runs larger than a simpler funnel. Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market or state. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or you cannot split the states cleanly, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or which market is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for deals that signed but never reached Permission To Operate, and for deals sitting in permitting or the interconnection queue with no recent activity, plus deals handed to a local contractor that have gone quiet. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between stages and between entities.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-activated-system by lead source and by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, the install partner, and the PTO stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
