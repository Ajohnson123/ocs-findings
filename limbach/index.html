When service leads cross 21 branches and two motions, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the crew on site. At Limbach's reach, an inbound service lead can come from any of 21 branches and several channels, and it splits into two very different paths: a same-week repair or maintenance call runs one way, a six-figure retrofit or replacement runs a completely different way, through a longer cycle with a facilities manager, a quote, and a dispatch and install schedule. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch the lead landed in, the channel that brought it in, the jump from a sold quote to a dispatched, scheduled crew. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a 21-branch, two-motion operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between branches, channels, and the dispatch handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries service leads across 21 branches and two very different motions (a same-week repair and a six-figure retrofit), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a service quote that waits on a facilities manager and goes cold, a lead routed to the wrong branch or owner and never picked back up, or a sold job that never cleanly hands from sales to a dispatched, scheduled crew. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across branches and channels, you cannot see which branch or channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted demand-gen spend never gets traced back. On the owner-direct side the stakes per lost lead are large, because a service relationship compounds over many years, so a single stalled one is worth far more than a single job. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads brought in and jobs actually delivered, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy 21-branch operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and many branches at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on recurring-service relationships the years add up fast.

Roughly what it costs (public-data estimate, scoped to the service side)

Built from public figures, and scoped on purpose to the owner-direct service motion only, not the whole company. The direct-to-owner service, maintenance, and retrofit revenue is treated as a conservative floor around $250M, which for an operation of this size likely understates it. A blended average job is taken around $120K, reflecting that retrofit and replacement projects run well into six figures while routine repairs and maintenance run far lower. Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound service lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by branch. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean branch tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which branch or channel is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for service quotes that went quiet waiting on a facilities manager or owner, and for sold jobs that never moved from sales into a dispatched, scheduled crew. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on the retrofit side each one is large.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-delivered-job by lead source, split by branch and by service versus project. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, branch, and the dispatch stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
