When one system routes leads to eighty-plus branches across three service lines, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the job getting done. At Truly Nolen's scale, an inbound lead has to land somewhere central and then route out to the right one of eighty-plus branches, in the right state, for the right service line: a quick residential pest visit runs one way, a commercial account runs another, a termite or WDO inspection runs a third, often with a longer cycle and a different crew. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: which branch the lead lands with, which channel brought it in, whether the branch closes the loop and reports back. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching all eighty-plus branches on one screen while every branch is busy running its own day. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-branch, multi-service-line operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between branch routing, service lines, and the follow-up loop, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot instance routes leads to eighty-plus branches across three service lines (residential pest, commercial pest, termite/WDO), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a lead assigned to the wrong branch and never picked back up, a commercial or termite inspection that goes quiet waiting on a callback, or a lead that never gets a clean source tag back to the branch and channel that generated it. They die quietly. And because lead source and branch are not tagged cleanly across the network, you cannot see which branch or which channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back to a branch or a campaign. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads generated and jobs actually serviced, and that single end-to-end view across eighty-plus branches is exactly what a busy branch network does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across many branches, several service lines, and several channels at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across eighty-plus branches the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by branch and by lead source. If a meaningful share come in as unassigned, unknown, or blank source, or with no clean branch tag, routing and attribution are blurry, and you cannot tell which branch or channel is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for commercial or termite inspections that went quiet without a logged follow-up, and for leads that never moved from first contact into a scheduled job. Count them by branch. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-serviced-job by branch and by lead source. If that report cannot be built cleanly because branch, service line, and lead source are not connected end to end, the view is blurred across the network, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
