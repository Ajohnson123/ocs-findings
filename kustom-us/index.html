A likely silent seam in Kustom US's lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I run the same core stack you do: HubSpot, inside a residential home-services operation. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools a field-services company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, built since 1968 into one of the largest privately owned property restoration platforms in the country, with more than 40 locations across more than 20 states, grown both organically and by folding in several acquired brands, carrying fire, water, mold, and storm work plus reconstruction and national large-loss response, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at Kustom's public footprint (that national branch network, that 24/7 emergency intake, that mix of fast residential mitigation and long commercial insurance reconstruction, those acquired brands being woven into one platform, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside, especially while several brands are still being merged into one platform. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When losses cross many branches, brands, and a long claim cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-location shop, one person can watch a loss from the first call to the final invoice. At a national, multi-brand platform, a loss can land at any hour, from any market, and split into two very different paths: a fast residential mitigation runs one way, and a larger or catastrophe loss runs a completely different way, through a longer cycle with an adjuster, an estimate, a reconstruction schedule, and a separate claim. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch and the brand the lead should route to, the channel that brought it in, the jump from emergency mitigation into the claim and the rebuild. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy with the next loss. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a national, multi-brand, mid-integration restoration platform creates.

The seam: leads that stall between branches, brands, intake, and the claim handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries 24/7 emergency intake across dozens of branches and several combined brands, and a lead has to cross from first notice of loss to the right branch and brand and then into the long insurance claim and reconstruction cycle, a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: a large-loss call logged to the wrong branch or brand and never picked back up, a mitigation job that never cleanly enters the reconstruction or claim queue, or an insurance estimate that goes quiet waiting on an adjuster. They die quietly. And because lead source, branch, and brand are not tagged cleanly across channels, you cannot see which branch, brand, or channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted intake and marketing spend never gets traced back. The stakes per lost lead are high, because commercial and large-loss restoration jobs are large-ticket, so even a tiny slip rate is steady money. The loss only shows up in the gap between losses called in and jobs actually completed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy national platform does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several branches, and several brands at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on a platform this size the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source, by branch, and by brand. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean branch or brand tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which branch, brand, or channel is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for mitigation jobs that never moved into a scheduled reconstruction or claim, and for estimates that went quiet waiting on an adjuster. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-completed-job by lead source, by branch, and by brand. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, branch, brand, and the completion stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
