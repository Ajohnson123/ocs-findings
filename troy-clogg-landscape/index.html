When leads cross two seasons, two job types, and 19 communities, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-season, single-job-type shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the job going in. At Troy Clogg's shape, a lead or a contract can come from any of 19-plus communities and split into paths that most home-services companies never have to reconcile: a commercial snow account signed in the fall runs one way, a residential design/build project signed in the spring runs a completely different way, and the two seasons themselves barely overlap on the calendar. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the community the lead came from, the season it arrived in, the jump from a snow-only client to a green-season prospect and back. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path across two seasons on one screen while everyone is busy running the current one. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a dual-season, multi-community operation creates.

The seam: leads and renewals that stall between seasons, service lines, and communities, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across two structurally opposite seasons (winter snow and ice, growing-season landscape and design/build) and two very different client types (commercial contract accounts, residential design/build) spread across 19-plus communities, a fraction of leads and renewals stall in the gaps: a snow client who is never offered a green-season contract, a design/build lead that goes quiet over the winter and is never followed back up, or a commercial account that lapses silently at renewal because no one owns the cross-season handoff. They die quietly. And because lead source and season are not tagged cleanly across two very different sales motions, you cannot see which channel or which community the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between contracts paid for and contracts actually renewed or cross-sold, and that single cross-season view is exactly what a busy 19-community operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead or a contract cleanly across two seasons, two service lines, and many communities at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across two full seasons a year the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every active or recent client grouped by service line (snow vs. green maintenance vs. design/build) and by community. If a meaningful share of snow-only clients show no green-season contact at all, or vice versa, the cross-sell handoff is not happening on purpose, it is just not happening.
  2. Filter for design/build leads that went quiet heading into winter, and for commercial accounts that lapsed at renewal with no logged reason. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the seasonal handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-closed-contract by lead source, split by season and by community. If that report cannot be built cleanly because season, service line, and community are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
