When leads cross three markets and run through a design-build cycle, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the project finishing. At FBC's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of three markets, and a remodel then runs through a long multi-team cycle: a sales consult, a design phase, a signed contract, production, and completion. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the market the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from marketing to sales, from sales to design, from design to production. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen across all three markets while everyone is busy closing the next project and keeping the current builds on schedule. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-market, multi-team design-build operation creates.

The seam: leads and projects that stall between markets, channels, and the team handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across three markets in three states and runs them through a multi-team design-build cycle (sales consult, design, contract, production, completion), a fraction of leads and projects stall in the gaps: a lead routed to the wrong market or rep and never picked back up, a closed sale that never cleanly enters the design queue, a design that stalls before production, or a warm showroom or referral lead that goes cold waiting on the next team. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and markets, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and projects actually built, and that single end-to-end view across three markets is exactly what a busy three-location operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several markets, and several team boundaries at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across three markets the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or location is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for closed sales that never entered the design queue, and for leads that never moved from sales into a scheduled project. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between teams.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-built-project by lead source, split by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, the design stage, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
