When leads cross three branches and a corporate layer, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-office shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the service being scheduled. At Economy Exterminators' reach, an inbound lead or a reschedule can land in any of three branch queues, Raleigh, Charlotte, or Wilmington, and a corporate layer of sales, marketing, operations, and IT sits above all three branch managers. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from a signed quote to a scheduled first service, especially on the larger commercial side. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path across three branches while everyone is busy running routes. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-branch, residential-and-commercial operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between branches, channels, and the contract handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across three branch offices (Raleigh/Triangle, Charlotte, Wilmington) and a corporate layer sits above them, a fraction of leads and reschedules stall in the gaps: a lead lands in the wrong branch queue and sits before anyone claims it, a commercial quote waits on a facilities contact and goes cold, or a signed contract never cleanly hands to a scheduled first service. They die quietly. And because lead source and branch are not tagged cleanly across channels and offices, you cannot see which office or which channel the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. On a recurring-service book, one lost account is not a single job, it is years of renewal revenue walking away quietly. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and accounts actually signed and serviced, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy three-branch operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and several branches at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on a recurring-service book the years compound.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by branch (Raleigh / Charlotte / Wilmington). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean branch tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or office is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for commercial quotes that went quiet waiting on a facilities contact, and for signed contracts that never moved into a scheduled first service. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, and on a recurring book each one represents years of revenue, not one job.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-signed-account by lead source, split by branch and residential vs. commercial. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, branch, and contract stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
