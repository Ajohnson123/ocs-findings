A likely silent seam in Integrity Home Exteriors' lead flow

One page. One seam. A rough number. Read time: 3 minutes.

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location, in-home-sales shape you run, just a messier version of it. So I spend my time on the silent seams between the tools an exteriors company runs on. You clearly run a real operation: 25 years out of Toledo, a second office in Farmington Hills, serving homeowners across Northwest Ohio and Southeast Michigan, with windows, siding, gutters, doors, and roofing all moving through one HubSpot. Looking only at Integrity's public footprint (that two-state reach, that full exteriors line, a high-volume lead engine all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a busy shop, even for a seasoned team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When leads cross two markets and a long in-home process, the handoffs are where they quietly stall.

At a single-market shop with one job type, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the install going in. At Integrity's reach, an inbound lead can come from either market and from any of several channels (a showroom visit, a Google review, paid search, a referral, the storm side), and then it has to survive a long in-home chain to become a built job: the inquiry, the inspection or in-home estimate, the measure, the sold job, and the production schedule. Every one of those steps is a handoff, and every handoff is a seam: the market the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the jump from an in-home estimate to a sold job, and the jump from sold to install. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy booking the next appointment. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a two-market, high-volume, multi-step residential operation creates.

The seam: leads that stall between markets, channels, and the in-home handoffs, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across two markets (Northwest Ohio and Southeast Michigan) and a long in-home chain (inquiry, in-home estimate, measure, sold job, install), a fraction of leads stall in the gaps: an in-home estimate that never gets a clean follow-up, a sold job that never cleanly enters the production queue, a lead routed to the wrong market or rep and never picked back up, or a warm review-driven inquiry that goes cold waiting on the next step. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and across the two states, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy two-market operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and two markets at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across a high-volume residential funnel the years add up fast.

Roughly what it costs (public-data estimate)

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market (Ohio / Michigan). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean market tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or which office is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for in-home estimates that never got a clean follow-up, and for sold jobs that never moved into a scheduled production / install. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff, between the appointment and the roof or windows actually going in.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-job by lead source, split by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, and the production stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
