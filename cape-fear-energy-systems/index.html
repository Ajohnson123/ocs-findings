A likely silent seam in Cape Fear Energy Systems' lead flow

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential home-services operation, the same field-services shape you run, just a messier version of it. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools an energy and home-services company runs on. You clearly run a real operation, an award-winning, NABCEP-certified solar and battery installer that Solar Power World has ranked number one in southeastern North Carolina, moving a customer from first inquiry through design, permitting, install, and utility interconnection, all on one HubSpot, across both the Carolinas. In solar, the long journey from signed to switched-on is where deals quietly stall.

At a one-truck shop, the same person sells the job and sees it through. At Cape Fear's size, a deal crosses several stages: marketing and sales bring it in from a referral, a past customer, the web, or an event and qualify it, then it has to move through design, permitting, install, inspection, and the utility-interconnection queue before it reaches Permission To Operate and becomes activated revenue. Every one of those handoffs is a seam, and the structure makes them sharper here than usual: sales and marketing sit on one side, while operations, permitting, and commercial install sit on another, and each deal's path depends on which market it is in (a Duke Energy job in North Carolina, a Dominion or co-op job in South Carolina, each county with its own permit office). Those handoffs are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy with the next customer. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that the solar process creates.

The seam: deals that stall between the signed contract and switched-on, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries every lead channel and every market (referral, past customer, web, event, and two states of utilities and permit offices) and a deal has to cross from sales and marketing through permitting, install, and utility interconnection, a fraction of deals stall in the gaps: a qualified inquiry that goes quiet after the first design conversation, a signed contract that stalls in permitting or the interconnection queue, or a warm referral that goes cold waiting on the next stage to pick it up. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels and markets, you cannot see which channel or which market the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted marketing and sales effort never gets traced back. The loss only shows up in the gap between deals signed and systems actually switched on, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a multi-market, multi-stage solar shop does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a deal cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, and two states of outside jurisdictions at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and on high-ticket solar and battery jobs the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by market. If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or you cannot split NC from SC cleanly, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or which market is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for deals that signed but never reached Permission To Operate, and for deals sitting in permitting or the interconnection queue with no recent activity, plus warm referrals gone quiet. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff between stages.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-activated-system by lead source and by market. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, market, and the PTO stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
