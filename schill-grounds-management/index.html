A likely silent seam in Schill's lead and job flow

I ran HubSpot inside a multi-state residential roofing operation, the same multi-location field-services shape Schill runs, just a messier version of it. Day to day, I map the silent seams between the tools a field-services company runs on, and the one I am about to show you is a pattern I lived, not one I am guessing at. You clearly run a real operation: founded in 1993, roughly thirty branches across five regions, more than sixteen acquisitions since 2020, doing both recurring commercial maintenance and project-based construction, all on one HubSpot. Looking only at Schill's public footprint (that thirty-branch reach, the two different work motions, all running on one HubSpot), one seam stands out as worth a two-minute check. It is the kind that is genuinely hard to see from inside a busy, fast-growing shop, even for a strong team. I have not seen your system. This is the pattern, and how to check it yourself.

When leads cross thirty branches and split into two kinds of work, the handoff is where they quietly stall.

At a single-branch shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the crew showing up. At Schill's reach, an inbound lead can come from any of thirty branches, and it then has to be sorted into one of two very different motions: a recurring maintenance contract, or a one-time design and build project. After it is sold, it has to hand again, from the salesperson to a crew schedule. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the branch the lead came from, the channel that brought it in, the division it belongs to, the jump from sales to a scheduled crew. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they do not, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next contract and onboarding the next acquired branch. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a multi-branch, two-division, roll-up operation creates.

The seam: leads and jobs that stall between branches, divisions, and the sales-to-crew handoff, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across thirty branches in five regions and splits them across two different work motions (recurring maintenance contracts and project-based construction), a fraction of leads and sold jobs stall in the gaps: a lead routed to the wrong branch or the wrong division and never picked back up, a maintenance renewal that quietly lapses, or a sold construction job that never cleanly hands to a scheduled crew. They die quietly. And because lead source is not tagged cleanly across channels, branches, and acquired brands, you cannot see which channel or which branch the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. Every new acquisition adds another branch with its own tagging habits, which widens the gap. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and jobs actually crewed and delivered, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy thirty-branch operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several channels, several branches, and two divisions at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across thirty branches the years add up fast.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by branch (location). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean branch or division tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or location is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for leads that never got routed to the right branch or division, for sold construction jobs that never moved into a scheduled crew, and for maintenance renewals that quietly lapsed. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-won-job by lead source, split by branch and by division. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, branch, division, and the crew-scheduling stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you would like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
