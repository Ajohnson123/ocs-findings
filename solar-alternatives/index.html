A likely silent seam in Solar Alternatives' lead flow

When leads cross seven state utility rules and three job paths, the stall is where they quietly go dark.

At a single-state, single-service shop, one person can watch a lead from the first call to the system going live. At Solar Alternatives' reach, an inbound lead can come from any of seven states, each with its own utility interconnection and net-metering process, and split into three very different paths: a residential solar-only install runs one way, a residential system with a Tesla Powerwall battery add-on runs another, and a commercial or industrial battery storage project runs a longer cycle with its own permitting, financing, and install scheduling. Every one of those handoffs is a seam: the state the lead came from, the job type it belongs to, the point where a permit or interconnection application is waiting on a utility. They are invisible when they work and invisible when they don't, because no single person is watching the whole path on one screen while everyone is busy closing the next job. That is not a competence gap. It is a structural blind spot that a seven-state, three-service operation creates.

The seam: leads and permits that stall between states, service lines, and utility interconnection, with attribution too blurry to see it

When one HubSpot carries leads across seven states (LA, TX, MS, AL, the FL panhandle, AR, TN), each with its own utility interconnection process, and three very different job types (residential solar-only, residential solar-plus-battery, commercial battery storage), a fraction of leads and in-process jobs stall in the gaps: a permit or interconnection application waiting on a specific state utility goes quiet, a lead that should route to the battery-attach upsell never gets flagged, or a commercial project never cleanly hands from sales to install scheduling. They go dark quietly. And because lead source and job type are not tagged cleanly across seven states and three service lines, you cannot see which state, channel, or service line the lost ones came from, so the leak never trips an alarm and the wasted spend never gets traced back. Because interconnection timelines already vary widely state to state, a stall that would stand out clearly in one state blends into normal variance across seven. The loss only shows up in the gap between leads paid for and systems actually installed, and that single end-to-end view is exactly what a busy multi-state operation does not naturally have.

This is the textbook definition of a seam: the point where one system is asked to carry a lead cleanly across several handoffs, several service lines, and several state utility processes at once, and a small fraction quietly falls through with no signal. A visible leak gets fixed same day. A silent one runs for years, and across seven states the years add up fast.

Roughly what it costs (public-data estimate)

Built from public figures (a 17-year, seven-state Gulf Coast solar and battery operation, Inc. 5000 honoree, 2,000+ monitored systems, 51-200 employees; revenue treated as a conservative floor around $15M, since small-business aggregator data of $1-5M is implausible at this employee count and system fleet and likely understates it; a blended average job around $28K, reflecting that residential solar-only runs lower, residential solar-plus-battery runs higher, and commercial battery jobs run higher still). Basis: ~$15M floor / ~$28K blended job = roughly 535 jobs a year, or about 1,785 inbound leads at a 30% close. Directional and estimate-grade, a floor meant to be made exact in an audit.

Check it yourself in two minutes

  1. In HubSpot, pull every inbound lead from the last 90 days grouped by lead source and by state (LA / TX / MS / AL / FL / AR / TN). If a meaningful share come in as unknown, direct, or blank source, or with no clean state tag, attribution is blurry, and you cannot tell which channel or state is actually paying for itself.
  2. Filter for permits or interconnection applications that went quiet waiting on a utility, and for leads that never moved from a residential quote into a battery-attach conversation or a scheduled install. Count them. Those are the ones stalling in the handoff.
  3. Ask HubSpot for cost-per-installed-system by lead source, split residential-only vs residential-plus-battery vs commercial. If that report cannot be built cleanly because source, job type, and permitting stage are not connected end to end, the view is blurred, and so is the leak.

If any of those is messier than you'd like, the seam is live, and the number above is no longer hypothetical.
